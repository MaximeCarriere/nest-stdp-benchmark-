\begin{abstract}
Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs) trained with Spike-Timing Dependent
Plasticity (STDP) offer a biologically plausible model of learning.
Their simulation, however, is substantially more expensive than
equivalent networks with fixed synapses.
We present a \emph{simulator-level} benchmark: we measure total
wall-clock runtime in NEST 3.6 when using its \texttt{stdp\_synapse}
model versus its \texttt{static\_synapse} model, across 2\,160
configurations (network size, connection density, firing rate, and
core count).
The results quantify the runtime overhead that NEST's STDP
implementation adds over static synapses under identical network
and input conditions---they do not isolate pure STDP arithmetic cost
from other simulator internals such as event scheduling or memory
management.
We find that \texttt{stdp\_synapse} can more than double total
simulation time, reaching 183\% overhead for networks of 8\,000
neurons.
Multi-core CPU parallelism reduces absolute runtime for both models
equally but does not close the overhead gap.
These results motivate a hardware-accelerated co-processor to
offload STDP weight updates from the CPU.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The human brain processes sensory information and learns from
experience using approximately $10^{11}$ neurons connected by
roughly $10^{15}$ synapses \cite{azevedo2009}.
Unlike the artificial neural networks behind today's deep-learning
revolution---which use smooth continuous activations and
gradient-based training—biological neurons communicate through
discrete electrical pulses called \emph{spikes}.
Models that respect this mechanism are called \emph{Spiking Neural
Networks} (SNNs).

SNNs are of great interest for neuromorphic computing, low-power
embedded intelligence, and fundamental neuroscience research.
However, simulating even a small fraction of biological-scale
networks on conventional computers remains extremely expensive.
A key bottleneck is the learning rule that governs how synaptic
connections are updated during simulation.

In this work we focus on the dominant biological learning rule:
\textbf{Spike-Timing Dependent Plasticity} (STDP).
We conduct a \emph{simulator-level} benchmark using the widely
used NEST simulator \cite{gewaltig2007}: we compare total wall-clock
simulation time when using NEST's \texttt{stdp\_synapse} model
against its \texttt{static\_synapse} model across a large parameter
sweep.
The benchmark measures the full simulator runtime consequence of
choosing one model over the other---including effects of NEST's
event scheduler, memory allocator, and threading
implementation---rather than isolating STDP arithmetic in isolation.
Our ultimate goal is to offload STDP weight updates to an
FPGA co-processor, enabling real-time simulation of large networks
at a fraction of the energy cost.

\section{Background: What is STDP?}
\label{sec:stdp}

\subsection{Synaptic plasticity}

Learning in biological neural circuits is implemented by changing
the \emph{strength} (weight) of synaptic connections.
A synapse connects a \emph{pre-synaptic} neuron
(the one sending a signal) to a \emph{post-synaptic} neuron
(the one receiving it).
The key insight from Hebbian theory is:
\emph{neurons that fire together wire together} \cite{hebb1949}.

\subsection{The STDP rule}

STDP refines this idea by making the weight change depend on the
\emph{relative timing} of pre- and post-synaptic spikes, first
demonstrated experimentally by Markram et al.\ \cite{markram1997}
and subsequently characterised in hippocampal neurons by
Bi and Poo \cite{bi1998}.

In NEST's \texttt{stdp\_synapse}, the rule is implemented via
pre- and post-synaptic eligibility traces.
Each pre-synaptic spike increments a trace $x_{\rm pre}$ that
decays with time constant $\tau_+$; each post-synaptic spike
increments a trace $x_{\rm post}$ decaying with $\tau_-$.
Synaptic weight $W$ is updated at every spike event:

\begin{equation}
  \text{post fires:}\quad \Delta W = +A_+\, x_{\rm pre}(t)
  \qquad\text{(LTP)}
  \label{eq:stdp_ltp}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
  \text{pre fires:}\quad \Delta W = -A_-\, x_{\rm post}(t)
  \qquad\text{(LTD)}
  \label{eq:stdp_ltd}
\end{equation}

\noindent For the additive rule ($\mu_{\pm}\!=\!0$), amplitudes are
$A_+\!=\!\lambda$ and $A_-\!=\!\lambda\alpha$, independent of the
current weight.
Weights are hard-clipped to $[0,\,W_{\max}]$.
The net effect is the classical exponential learning window
(Fig.~\ref{fig:stdp}): potentiation when pre fires before post
($\Delta t\!>\!0$, LTP), depression in the reverse order ($\Delta
t\!<\!0$, LTD).

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/paper_fig1_stdp_intro.pdf}
  \caption{%
    \textbf{STDP learning window.}
    \textbf{(A)} Weight change $\Delta W$ as a function of the
    inter-spike interval $\Delta t$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:stdp}).
    Pre fires before post ($\Delta t > 0$): synapse strengthens (LTP,
    green).
    Post fires before pre ($\Delta t < 0$): synapse weakens (LTD, red).
    \textbf{(B)} Schematic of a spike pair.
    The synapse weight $W$ is updated after every spike pair
    according to their relative timing.
  }
  \label{fig:stdp}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Expected simulation cost of \texttt{stdp\_synapse}}

When a pre-synaptic neuron fires, its spike is delivered to each of
its $k_{\rm out}\!=\!pN$ post-synaptic targets, triggering a weight
update at each synapse (Eqs.~\ref{eq:stdp_ltp}--\ref{eq:stdp_ltd}).
Because every neuron fires at mean rate $\nu$, the total weight-update
rate scales as:
\[
  \text{Updates/s} \;=\; \nu \cdot p \cdot N^2
\]
This is \emph{quadratic} in $N$: doubling the network size quadruples
the update workload.
With $N\!=\!2\,000$, $\nu\!=\!20$\,Hz, and $p\!=\!0.1$, this yields
$\approx\!8 \times 10^6$ weight updates per second.
Each update involves reading the current weight, evaluating the trace
$x_{\rm pre}$ or $x_{\rm post}$, computing the new weight, and
writing it back---operations that must all be serialised on a CPU and
whose aggregate memory-access cost grows super-linearly with $N$,
as shown below.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Model description}

Following the structured description format of Nordlie et al.\
\cite{nordlie2009}, Table~\ref{tab:model} summarises all model
components.

\begin{table}[h]
\caption{Model summary (Nordlie et al.\ format).}
\label{tab:model}
\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}ll@{}}
\hline
\textbf{Component} & \textbf{Description} \\
\hline
Populations    & $N$ neurons, single excitatory population \\
Neuron model   & \texttt{iaf\_psc\_alpha} (LIF, alpha-function
                 synaptic currents) \\
Connectivity   & Pairwise Bernoulli, probability $p$; no autapses \\
Synapse models & \texttt{static\_synapse} or \texttt{stdp\_synapse} \\
External input & \texttt{poisson\_generator} $\to$ all neurons,
                 rate $\nu$ \\
Recordings     & Spike times; wall-clock simulation time \\
Simulator      & NEST 3.6, resolution 0.1\,ms \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\paragraph{Neuron model.}
Each neuron follows leaky integrate-and-fire dynamics:
\begin{equation}
  \tau_m \frac{dV}{dt} = -(V - E_L) + R_m\bigl(I_e + I_{\rm syn}(t)\bigr)
  \label{eq:lif}
\end{equation}
with threshold $V_{\rm th}$, reset $V_r$, and refractory period
$\tau_{\rm ref}$.
Synaptic currents $I_{\rm syn}$ have an alpha-function time course.
A constant bias current $I_e\!=\!400$\,pA drives spontaneous firing.
All other parameters take NEST 3.6 default values for
\texttt{iaf\_psc\_alpha}.

\paragraph{Connectivity.}
Following Senk et al.\ \cite{senk2022}, connectivity is drawn by
a \emph{pairwise Bernoulli} process: for each ordered pair of
distinct neurons $(j,\,i)$ with $j \ne i$, a directed connection
$j\!\to\!i$ is created independently with probability $p$.
Autapses are excluded; at most one connection exists between any
ordered pair (no multapses).
The expected in-degree is $k_{\rm in}\!=\!pN$ with coefficient of
variation $1/\sqrt{pN}$.

\paragraph{Synapse models.}
Two conditions are compared:
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{2pt}
  \item \texttt{static\_synapse}: weight $W\!=\!0.5$, delay
        $d\!=\!0.1$\,ms; weights are fixed throughout simulation.
  \item \texttt{stdp\_synapse}: additive pair-based STDP
        ($\mu_{\pm}\!=\!0$); $\tau_+\!=\!\tau_-\!=\!20$\,ms,
        $\lambda\!=\!0.01$, $\alpha\!=\!1.2$,
        $W_{\max}\!=\!1.0$, initial $W\!=\!0.5$,
        delay $d\!=\!0.1$\,ms.
        Weights are updated at every pre- and post-synaptic spike
        event according to Eqs.~\ref{eq:stdp_ltp}--\ref{eq:stdp_ltd}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Parameter sweep and timing protocol}

The benchmark covers:
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0pt}
  \item Network size $N\in\{10,\,50,\,100,\,200,\,500,\,1000,\,2000,
        \,4000,\,8000,\,16000\}$
  \item CPU cores: 1, 2, 4, 8
  \item Connection probability $p\in\{0.05,\,0.10,\,0.20\}$
  \item Poisson input rate $\nu\in\{10,\,20,\,40\}$\,Hz
  \item Synapse model: \texttt{static\_synapse} vs.\
        \texttt{stdp\_synapse}
\end{itemize}

Each configuration was simulated for 20\,s of biological time
(resolution 0.1\,ms).
Wall-clock time was recorded with \texttt{time.perf\_counter()}
at \texttt{nest.Simulate()} entry and exit, isolating the simulation
phase from the network-setup phase; all reported times refer to the
simulation phase only.
Each condition was repeated three times; results are reported as mean
$\pm$ standard error of the mean (SEM).
In total 2\,160 simulations were executed over 2.9\,days on a desktop
workstation running Arch Linux (x86-64).

\textbf{Note on trial independence.}
The \texttt{static\_synapse} and \texttt{stdp\_synapse} conditions
for the same nominal configuration were assigned independent random
seeds (NEST \texttt{rng\_seed}), so the realised network topology
and Poisson spike trains differ between conditions.
Because connectivity is drawn by a pairwise Bernoulli process with
expected synapse count $pN^2$ and $\mathrm{CV}\!=\!1/\sqrt{pN^2}$,
topology variability is below 1\% for $N\!\ge\!100$ and is
negligible relative to the measured runtime differences.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{\texttt{stdp\_synapse} runtime overhead is negligible
below 1\,000 neurons, then becomes critical}

Fig.~\ref{fig:timing} compares simulation time for static and STDP
networks as a function of network size, at 1 core, $p\!=\!0.1$,
and 20\,Hz.
For small networks ($N \leq 500$) the overhead is negligible:
both rules complete in under 5\,s and the bars are nearly identical
(Fig.~\ref{fig:timing}A).
The linear-scale plot in Fig.~\ref{fig:timing}B reveals a sharp
transition around $N\!\approx\!1\,000$ neurons, above which the two
curves diverge dramatically.
At $N\!=\!2\,000$ STDP takes $1.9\times$ longer (53\,s vs.\ 28\,s);
at $N\!=\!4\,000$ it reaches $2.3\times$ (177\,s vs.\ 76\,s);
and at $N\!=\!8\,000$ it takes $2.4\times$ longer
(808\,s vs.\ 330\,s)---an absolute overhead of nearly
\textbf{8 minutes per simulation}.

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/paper_fig2_simple_timing.pdf}
  \caption{%
    \textbf{Static vs.\ STDP simulation time.}
    \textbf{(A)} Bar chart for small networks.
    \textbf{(B)} Full log-scale comparison; shaded region marks the
    STDP overhead.
    Each point is the mean of 3 trials; error bars show SEM.
    Conditions: 1 core, $p\!=\!0.1$, 20\,Hz Poisson input.
  }
  \label{fig:timing}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Overhead peaks around 4\,000 neurons then partially
stabilises}

Fig.~\ref{fig:overhead}A shows the STDP overhead (relative to
static, \%) as a function of $N$ for three connection densities.
For $p\!=\!0.1$ the overhead is below 5\% up to $N\!=\!100$,
then rises steeply, exceeding \textbf{50\%} at $N\!\approx\!500$
and surpassing \textbf{100\%} at $N\!\approx\!1\,000$.
The maximum recorded overhead is $\mathbf{183\%}$ at
$N\!=\!4\,000$ ($p\!=\!0.2$, 10\,Hz): a 183\% overhead means
STDP takes $2.83\times$ \emph{as long} as the static baseline
(total$\,=\,$static$\,+\,$183\%$\,\times\,$static).
Note that at the highest firing rate (40\,Hz) and density
($p\!=\!0.2$), the percentage overhead \emph{decreases} for the
largest networks because the static simulation itself becomes
expensive; however, the absolute overhead remains hundreds of
seconds.

Fig.~\ref{fig:overhead}B plots the STDP overhead \emph{per synapse}.
Rather than staying constant (ideal dashed line), the cost per
synapse increases with $N$, revealing super-linear behaviour.
The per-synapse cost does not stay constant as the network grows,
suggesting that memory-access patterns become a limiting factor.
One plausible explanation, consistent with the observed inflection
around $N\!\approx\!800$, is that the synapse table at that point
exceeds the processor's fast on-chip cache, leading to more costly
memory accesses; however, we did not collect low-level profiling
counters (e.g.\ cache-miss rates) to confirm this directly.

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering

  \caption{%
    \textbf{\texttt{stdp\_synapse} runtime overhead scaling.}
    \textbf{(A)} Percentage overhead of \texttt{stdp\_synapse} relative

    probabilities (1 core, 20\,Hz).
    Dashed lines mark 50\% and 100\% overhead thresholds.
    \textbf{(B)} \texttt{stdp\_synapse} overhead per synapse; the

  }

\end{figure}

\subsection{Multi-core parallelism does not close the \texttt{stdp\_synapse} runtime gap}

A natural first response to the \texttt{stdp\_synapse} runtime overhead
is to use more CPU cores: if \texttt{stdp\_synapse} doubles the
simulation time, running on two cores might restore it.
Fig.~\ref{fig:multicore} tests this directly.
\textbf{Speedup with more cores (Panel A).}
For static synapses, parallelism is beneficial only for large
networks.
Below $N\!=\!500$ neurons, adding cores barely helps or even hurts
performance: coordinating multiple threads requires constant
communication between them, and for small workloads this coordination
cost exceeds the time saved by splitting the work.
At 10 neurons, 8 cores are actually $1.8\times$ \emph{slower} than a
single core.
For large networks ($N \ge 2\,000$), multi-core scaling is
substantial, reaching close to or above the ideal linear line.
The practical ceiling for this hardware configuration sits around
$10\times$ speedup with 8 cores.

\textbf{The \texttt{stdp\_synapse} runtime gap persists at every core count (Panel B).}
Panel B shows wall-clock simulation time for both synapse models at
$N\!=\!4\,000$ neurons.
Both curves decrease steeply as cores are added: going from 1 to 8
cores reduces \texttt{static\_synapse} time from 76\,s to 7.8\,s
($9.7\times$) and \texttt{stdp\_synapse} time from 177\,s to 16\,s
($11.0\times$).

The two curves descend in parallel, keeping approximately the same
proportional distance at every core count ($2.1$--$2.3\times$, as
indicated by the bracket at 8 cores).
More formally, if $T_{\text{stdp}}(N,k) \approx r \cdot
T_{\text{static}}(N,k)$ for an overhead ratio $r$ approximately
independent of core count $k$, then the speedup of each model
relative to its own single-core baseline is identical---the factor
$r$ cancels in $T(N,1)/T(N,k)$.
Parallelism therefore reduces absolute runtime for both models by
the same proportion, leaving both the overhead ratio and the absolute
time gap unchanged.

The implication is direct: adding CPU cores is insufficient to
eliminate the \texttt{stdp\_synapse} runtime penalty.
At 8 cores---already a significant hardware investment---the overhead
remains $2.1\times$.
An architectural solution that reduces the per-synapse update cost
itself, rather than distributing it across more processors, is
required.

  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/paper_fig4_multicore.pdf}
  \caption{%
    \textbf{Multi-core scaling.}
    \textbf{(A)} Speedup relative to 1 core for different network
    sizes (static synapses, $p\!=\!0.1$, 20\,Hz); dashed line is
    ideal linear scaling.
    Small networks ($\le\!500$ neurons) gain little or nothing from
    additional cores.
    \textbf{(B)} Absolute wall-clock simulation time for static and
    STDP synapses at $N\!=\!4\,000$.
    Both rules scale similarly with core count (faint dashed lines
    show ideal scaling from each 1-core baseline), but the STDP
    overhead of $\sim\!2.1\times$ persists regardless of how many
    cores are used.
    More cores speed up both rules equally---they do not close
    the gap.
  }

\end{figure}

\section{The Case for FPGA Acceleration}
\label{sec:fpga}

The results above identify a clear performance bottleneck:
STDP weight updates incur a super-linearly growing cost that
neither multi-core CPUs nor software-level optimisation can
fully address.
The per-synapse timing data (Fig.~\ref{fig:overhead}B) is
consistent with a memory-hierarchy bottleneck on a general-purpose
CPU, where each update requires the processor to:

\begin{enumerate}\setlength\itemsep{0pt}
  \item \textbf{Fetch} the synapse record (potentially from slow
        main memory for large tables),
  \item \textbf{Compute} the exponential decay $e^{-\Delta t/\tau}$,
  \item \textbf{Write back} the updated weight—millions of times
        per second.
\end{enumerate}

Directly profiling cache-miss rates and memory-bandwidth utilisation
would be needed to confirm this interpretation; we leave that as
future work.
Nevertheless, the empirical scaling behaviour motivates an
architectural approach that avoids this bottleneck by design.

FPGAs (Field-Programmable Gate Arrays) offer a compelling
alternative because:
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0pt}
  \item \textbf{Massive parallelism}: an FPGA can process hundreds of
        synaptic updates simultaneously in dedicated hardware lanes.
  \item \textbf{On-chip BRAM}: synapse tables for moderate networks
        can fit in fast on-chip block RAM, potentially avoiding the
        memory-latency bottleneck hypothesised above.
  \item \textbf{Fixed-point exponentials}: the STDP kernel
        (Eq.~\ref{eq:stdp}) maps efficiently to lookup tables.
  \item \textbf{Energy efficiency}: FPGA implementations of neural
        simulation consume 10–100$\times$ less power than GPU or CPU
        equivalents \cite{cheung2012,han2020}.
  \item \textbf{Deterministic latency}: unlike CPUs, an FPGA pipeline
        has cycle-accurate timing, simplifying the spike-timestamp
        bookkeeping required by STDP.
\end{itemize}

Several FPGA-based SNN accelerators have been proposed
\cite{cheung2012,sripad2020,han2020}, but none directly targets the
STDP update pipeline as a co-processor to a CPU-based simulator
such as NEST.
Our planned architecture places the FPGA alongside the host CPU:
the CPU manages network topology and spike routing, while the FPGA
handles all weight-update arithmetic.
The benchmarks presented here will guide the memory-bandwidth and
arithmetic-throughput requirements for that co-processor design.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have systematically characterised the simulator-level runtime
overhead of NEST's \texttt{stdp\_synapse} model relative to
\texttt{static\_synapse}, across a wide range of network sizes,
connection densities, and hardware configurations.
Key findings:

\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0pt}
  \item \textbf{Below $\sim\!1\,000$ neurons}: the runtime overhead
        of \texttt{stdp\_synapse} is negligible ($<\!10\%$); standard
        CPUs are sufficient.
  \item \textbf{Above $\sim\!1\,000$ neurons}: a sharp transition
        occurs---the overhead exceeds 50\% by $N\!=\!500$ and peaks
        at \textbf{183\%} ($2.83\times$ as long) around 4\,000 neurons.
  \item The absolute runtime penalty at 8\,000 neurons is nearly 8
        minutes per simulation---prohibitive for parameter sweeps.
  \item CPU multi-core parallelism reduces absolute runtime
        substantially (up to $10\times$ at 8 cores for large $N$),
        but does not reduce the \texttt{stdp\_synapse} overhead ratio,
        which remains $2.1$--$2.3\times$ at all core counts.
  \item The per-synapse cost scaling is consistent with a
        memory-hierarchy effect (synapse table size relative to
        on-chip cache), though direct profiling would be needed to
        confirm this---making FPGA on-chip BRAM a well-motivated
        target for future investigation.
\end{itemize}

These results establish a quantitative baseline for the design and
evaluation of an FPGA-based STDP accelerator, which is the next
step of this work.
